In the face of a rapidly changing climate, understanding how shifts in global temperature and precipitation influence public health has never been more vital. This data story explores the intricate relationships between climate anomalies and disease patterns, offering a global-to-local perspective. By examining temperature and rainfall changes across decades, and aligning these trends with outbreaks of diseases like influenza and dengue, this dashboard reveals insights into the environmental conditions that shape health outcomes. Using interactive visualizations and real-world datasets, we investigate not only where and how the planet is changing—but also how these changes ripple through human populations.

Warming and Wetter: How Earth's Climate is Changing?

Through a series of interactive visualizations, this section explores evolving patterns of temperature and precipitation across regions and decades. From choropleth maps to bar and ridgeline plots, we track how the planet's surface has warmed and rainfall patterns have shifted over time. These visualizations reveal that climate change is not a uniform experience—its impact varies greatly across geographies and historical periods. By visualizing these changes, we lay a critical foundation for understanding their ripple effects on global health.

Global Monthly Surface Temperature

The Global Monthly Surface Temperature Choropleth Map highlights Earth's seasonal cycles. We observe that July is summer in the Northern Hemisphere, showing widespread warming over North America, Europe, and Asia, while it is winter in the Southern Hemisphere. This seasonal pattern is consistent annually, yet masks the larger signal: a steady upward shift in global temperatures over time.

Temperature Anomaly Over Time

Temperature Distribution by Decade

The Temperature Anomaly Over Time plot shows how average global temperatures have diverged from the 1951–1980 baseline. From the 1940s to the late 1970s, global temperatures were consistently below baseline, indicating a period of relative cooling. Around 1980, however, we see a dramatic shift: temperature anomalies begin rising steadily, crossing the zero line around the year 2000. This marks a critical threshold—global temperatures are no longer fluctuating around the baseline; they are now consistently higher. The most recent years show anomalies reaching +0.7°C or more, signaling a total warming of approximately 1.5°C from the coldest decades.

This temporal warming trend is further confirmed in the Temperature Distribution Over Time strip plot, which compares regional temperature distributions in the 1950s versus the 2010s. Every region—Asia, Europe, North America, Africa, Oceania, and even Antarctica—exhibits a clear rightward shift in temperature, indicating widespread warming. Particularly striking are:

  • Asia and Europe, where the 2010s distributions are almost entirely separated from their 1950s baselines.
  • North America and Africa, which show broad warming with increased variability.
  • Antarctica, where warming is evident but shows greater spread and unpredictability.
  • Oceania, which despite its smaller landmass, demonstrates a noticeable shift.

    • In summary, these visualizations collectively demonstrate that climate change is not isolated to specific regions or timeframes. It is a global, accelerating phenomenon, with consistent warming patterns observable across hemispheres, decades, and continents. The data leaves little room for doubt: Earth’s climate system has fundamentally shifted, with rising temperatures now defining the new global norm.

    Global Annual Precipitation

    The Global Monthly Precipitation Choropleth Map reveals a consistent spatial trend: areas near the equator receive substantially more rainfall than regions at higher latitudes. This is due to the Intertropical Convergence Zone (ITCZ), where warm, moist air rises and cools, producing persistent rain. This pattern is especially prominent over equatorial Africa, Southeast Asia, and parts of South America. In contrast, subtropical zones—such as Northern Africa, Australia, and the Middle East—tend to remain dry. The map underscores how geography and atmospheric circulation shape precipitation intensity across the globe.

    Precipitation Anomaly Over Time

    Precipitation Distribution by Decade

    The Precipitation Anomaly Over Time plot paints a more erratic picture than temperature. From the 1940s through the late 1970s, global precipitation anomalies were mostly negative, indicating a period of drier-than-average conditions. Around 1980, however, there is a notable shift: anomalies begin swinging into positive territory. The decades between 1990 and 2010 exhibit several extremely wet years, with anomalies exceeding +20mm to +25mm above baseline.

    The Regional Precipitation Distribution plot further illustrates how rainfall shifts are regionally diverse. Unlike the globally consistent temperature increase, precipitation changes vary dramatically:

  • Africa shows heightened variability, with both more extreme wet years and pronounced dry periods.
  • Asia presents a mixed pattern—some regions experienced drying, while others show increases in precipitation.
  • Europe reveals relatively modest changes between decades, with slight signs of increased rainfall extremes.
  • Oceania is marked by high variability in both periods, reflecting its sensitivity to oceanic and atmospheric cycles such as El Niño.

    • Across nearly all regions, the spread of precipitation anomalies has widened, indicating a rise in extreme rainfall and drought events. The “World” distribution captures this shift: while the 1950s skewed dry, the 2010s show slightly wetter and more variable conditions.

    Disease Dynamics in a Changing World

    With the rhythm of a shifting climate established, we now focus on its effect on human health. How are diseases responding to environmental stress? This section spotlights influenza and dengue — two of the world’s most widespread and climate-sensitive diseases. Through seasonal and strain-based visualizations, we uncover how flu patterns fluctuate between hemispheres and consider whether seasonal predictability is being eroded. This section challenges us to reflect on the vulnerability of global health systems in a warming, interconnected world.

    From Weather to Wards: Exploring Correlations

    We move from observation to connection—are rising temperatures and increased rainfall truly linked to disease outbreaks? In this section, scatterplots allow us to analyze the relationships between temperature and influenza, and precipitation and dengue. What emerges is a compelling insight: warmer regions often see influenza spikes, while rainy seasons correspond with dengue outbreaks. While correlation is not causation, these trends provide valuable cues for epidemiological forecasting and underscore the need for climate-informed health policies. This section serves as a critical link—connecting environmental shifts to real-world health consequences.

    Temperature vs. Influenza Cases

    This scatter plot shows a strong inverse correlation between temperature and influenza cases. Peak flu counts (>2,000 cases) occur at 0–15°C, with a sharp drop above 25°C. Patterns are consistent in both hemispheres—Northern and Southern—supporting cold weather as a major transmission driver.

    Dengue cases cluster around 1,500–2,000 mm annual rainfall. Transmission is highest at moderate precipitation—enough for mosquito breeding, but not extreme. Very low (<1,000mm) or very high (3,500mm+) rainfall regions show fewer cases, likely due to habitat limits.

    Climate Trends in Malaysia

    Malaysia Climate Cases over Time

    Malaysia’s dengue trends closely track its tropical rainfall cycles. While temperatures stay stable (26–31°C), precipitation peaks in October–December and dips in June–July. Dengue cases rise about 1–2 months after wet periods, with surges in January–March, and drop during the dry season. The heatmap shows clear seasonal patterns and year-to-year shifts, highlighting how climate timing can guide timely public health action.
